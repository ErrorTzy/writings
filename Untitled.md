Two Readings of "everyone could rationally will"

I think the problems you raised in class, namely whether slave owners can rationally will there to be slaves, depend on a specific (and most natural) reading of "everyone could rationally will".

On this reading, we take this phrase to be literal. To test whether everyone could rationally will something, we are supposed to throw away all of our moral intuitions and ask ourselves: For any rational creature, can this creature will the principle "let there be slaves"? The clause "For any rational creature" is doing the hard work here. This is because if we drop this clause, then any principle can be willed by some creature. And the principle that picks out "rational creature" is the wide view about rationality. If we want the theory to say that slave-owning is wrong, we must say anything that can will the principle of slavery is irrational. And therefore we must define the wide view of rationality in some way such that it take the slave owners who can will this principle as irrational.

This procedure also works for several other similar counterexamples. For example, in some previous class I remeber we talked about the problem whether a criminal can rationally will the principle that "all criminals should be punished". The move made was to say those who can will the principle of criminal punishment are irrational. 

But this kind of ad hoc definition of rationality is unsatisfactory for me. In fact, as a subjectivist about rationality, I believe any consistant rule-following action count as rational. Therefore I suggest another reading of "everyone could rationally will"

The reason Parfit made the modification from "you could rationally will" to "everyone could rationally will" is exactly to deal with the problem that you can rationally will to have slaves if you are a slave owner. As I claimed, your psychological capacity to will this principle depends on the de se belief that "I am a slave owner". And the modification to "everyone" is supposed to work as a veil of ignorance that blocks the kind of capacity to will a principle that is dependent on one's de se belief. In other words, my suggestion of a second reading will be something closer to "I could rationally will the principle where I might be everyone".

The first reading requires us to throw away our moral intuitions, but do not throw away any de se belief. We are asked to consider what a slave owner would think, and the slave owner is allowed to know that he is a slave owner. The consequence is that we need the wide principle to compensate the moral intuition that has been thrown away, in order to get a distinction between willable and unwillable principle. And this will make the wide view ad hoc.

On the second reading, it asks us to throw away our de se beliefs, but keep one's own moral intuitions. Given that my moral intuition is that slavery is undesirable (under the veil of ignorance), then I cannot believe that "everyone could rationally will" the principle of slavery. The consequence is that we may end up with subjectivism, if people can have different moral intuitions. (That said, it is not inconsistent with objectivism, because one can ad hoc define some wide view about "moral intuition" and rule out some intuition as being "immoral intuition".) Anyway, I think this second reading solves the problem you raised in class, relative to my moral intuitions.

The difference between these two readings is that the second reading allows something to be rational and immoral at the same time. Maybe eventually it will become a verbal dispute between the meaning of "rational" and "moral". But according to my way of using these two words, it is possible for something to be rational and immoral at the same time.
