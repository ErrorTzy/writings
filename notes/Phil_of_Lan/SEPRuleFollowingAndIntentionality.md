## 3. The Skeptical Solution

What is a skeptical solution?

- A *straight* solution: identifying some meaning-constituting fact
- A *skeptical* solution: there is no such fact, but deny this leads to eliminating our practice of ascribing meaning

> Comment: Notice that, any straight solution formulated in language might be interpreted in a quus manner. If so, then if one is willing to insist the skpticism, no one can force her to accept the straight solution.

Two options for the skeptical solution:

1. error theory
2. non-factualism

### 3.1. Error Theory

**Elimantivism**: Don't have to eliminate meaning. One can say that there are no facts about meaning, but still agrees there are pragmatic virtues in saying meaning-constituting facts exist.

> Comment: Error theory, in general, demand us to (1) believe that P, and (2) believe that P is false. This seems to be uncanny, though it might be possible if we allow our belief in P is caused by pragmatic considerations.

**Example**: the pragmtic virtues of ascribing meaning is to allow us to agree with the community with respect to inclinations to "go on" in certain ways; This would in turn enable us to get 5 apples when we want to get 5 apples.

**Objection**: Error theory must be cashed out by presupposing there is a fact about whether agreeing or letting inclination to go on. If we deny there is such a thing as norm compliance, then we cannot even formulate error theory

> Comment: The objection seems wrong because one can admit the agreement of behavior and argue that behavior does not amount to meaning. Basically error theorists are agreeing with kripke about dispostionalism falling short of capturing meaning, but using the resource of dispositionalism to cash out error theory.
