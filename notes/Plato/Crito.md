The key tension:

- Socrates chooses to die because he respected the trial, which is determined by the opinion of the many.
- Socrates persuades Crito to disrespect the opinion of the many.

The reason why Crito wants to save Socrates:

1. Crioo would be deprived of such a companion (44c)
2. It is shameful for Crito to be seen as someone who regard money to be more important than friends (44c)
3. Socrates' death would satisfy the enemies of Socrates (45d)
4. Socrates' death would leave his children without a father (45d)

There are two separate justifications: (1) Crito is justified to save Socrates, and (2) Socrates should escape. 1-2 are about Crito's justification, 3-4 are about Socrates' justification.

Reason for disrespecting the opinion of the many:

1. they are incapable of making someone either prudent ot imprudent, but do whatever they happen to do by chance (44d)
2. one should hornor the opinion of the prudent expert, not the imprudent (47a)

The only way to make sense of Socrates' position is to say experts are determined by knowledge. If who counts as an expert is determined by the opinion of the many, then Socrates' argument undermines itself.

Crito's reason: the many can kill us if we don't obey (48b)

Socrates: this is a reason for living, not living well (48b); 

1. An action is justified iff it aims at living well; 
2. living well is equivalent to living justly
3. Therefore, an action is justified iff it aims at living justly.

Proof: Socrates should not escape

1. If an action is unjust, then it is never justified (49a)
2. If an reaction to injustice is also unjust, then this reaction is never justified (49c-d)

This is begging the question. Why reaction to injustice does not give justification? Plato is not allowed to say an action is unjust simply because of the action itself, because action is just or unjust with respect to its purpose or aim.
