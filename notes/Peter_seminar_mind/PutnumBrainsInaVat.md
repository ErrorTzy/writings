## Magical Theory of Reference

**Proposition 2**: Having a mental image of a tree is not sufficient for a representation of a tree

**Proof**: Alien humans have never seen tree see a picture of a tree. They do not know what that is. Therefore, the mental image "only represents a strange object,... not a representation of a tree." 

> But the strange object IS a tree!!! What are you talking about? It's like saying that the representation of hespherus is not a representation of Venus.

**Objection**: The mental image is a tree because the image is a representation of a tree. There is a strange causal pathway between the tree and the mental image.

> Here, Putnum is already assuming the causal theory of representation. But to resist that mental image being insufficient, one does not need to accept the causal theory.

**Defense**: The picture can be purely created by accident, so no tree is involved in the causal pathway that produces the mental image. Therefore, the mental image of a tree is not a representation of a tree.

> [!Objection]
> 
> The conclusion does not follow. Putnum confuses two questions:
> 
> - Does the mental image of a tree represents a tree? And 
> - Does the mental image of a tree justifies the existence of a tree?
> 
> These are separate questions because we can surely have a mental picture of fictional characters.
> 
> Putnum's argument only shows that the mental image of a tree does not give us justification of the existence of a tree, since we may get the mental image just by an accidental result of spilled paints. His argument does not show that the mental image of a tree is not a representation of a tree.
> 
> Therefore I insist that mental image of a tree is a representation of a tree. It's just we may or may not attach ontological commitments to that representation.
> 
> That said, it might be a problem when we try to ask whether the tree being represented is the actual tree. That is a problem, since if we follow the causal path of the representation there is no tree there. But it seems all we can say is that it is unclear whether these two are the same tree. Consider some gettier case:
> 
> Assume that I learned the name from you when I heard you say "Donald Trump is the president of the United States." However, in fact you were saying "Joe Biden is the president of the United States." and I heard it wrong, due to my hearing problem. Now I picked up the name "Donald Trump" and start to use it. Does my use of "Donald Trump", learned from illusion, refer to the actual trump? 

**Proposition 3**: The same thing is true of words: speaking without understanding does not amount to representation, even with the feeling of understanding.

> I don't understand what Putnum is talking about when Putnum says one have the feeling of understanding and can fool a Japanese telepath, but does not actually understand the meaning of the words.

**Proposition 4**: Given 2 and 3, speaking words with mental image is not sufficient for representation. Therefore, though about words and mental pictures do not intrinsically represent anything.

## The case of the brains in a vat

**Problem**: If we were brains in a vat, could we say or think that we were?

**Proposition**: If it is the case that we are brains in a vat, "we are brains in a vat" is necessarily false.

**Proof**: If we are brains in a vat, then although we can say words "we are brains in a vat", we cannot refer to brains and vats.

## Turing's test

**Problem**: Is Turing Test for Reference a definitive test for shared reference?

**Answer**: No, since it is logically possible for a person to pass the test without actually refering to anything. This is because "it seems evident that we cannot and should not attribute reference to such a device" because "none of these sentences is at all connected to the real world". 

We are able to refer because we have language introduction rules that can move from experience to language, and language elimination rules that moves from language to action. Syntactic play is not sufficient for reference.
