In philosophy, when people disagree with each other, they are often also disagreeing about the meanings of words. For example, when Kantians and utilitarians disagree about what is right or wrong, they are at the same time disagreeing about the meanings of "right" and "wrong". The hard question, however, is whether they are *merely* disagreeing about words.

It is not entirely clear what counts as a *merely* verbal dispute, but some clear cases help illustrate the idea. The most famous example comes from William James: imagine a squirrel moving around a tree while a man also moves around the tree, always keeping his face turned toward the squirrel. The question is whether the man is going around the squirrel. According to James, this is a merely verbal dispute:

> Which party is right depends on what you practically mean by ‘going round’ the squirrel. If you mean passing from the north of him to the east, then to the south, then to the west, and then to the north of him again, obviously the man does go round him, for he occupies these successive positions. But if on the contrary you mean being first in front of him, then on the right of him then behind him, then on his left, and finally in front again, it is quite as obvious that the man fails to go round him... Make the distinction, and there is no occasion for any farther dispute. (James 1907, 25)
